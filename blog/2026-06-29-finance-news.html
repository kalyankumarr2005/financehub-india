Market Summary: Equities Dip, Bonds Rally Amid Mixed Cues

The Indian benchmark indices experienced a downturn today, largely influenced by renewed concerns over US-Iran tensions and a wave of profit booking after recent gains. The Sensex ended the day 372 points, or 0.48%, lower at 76,728.37, while the Nifty 50 settled at 23,946.25, down 110 points, or 0.46%. This brought the Nifty 50 just below the crucial 23,950 mark, signaling a cautious mood among investors.

The broader market also felt the pressure, with the Nifty Midcap 100 index dropping 0.37% and the Smallcap 100 index declining by a more significant 0.62%. This indicates that the selling pressure was not confined to large-cap stocks but extended across the market spectrum.

Prominent heavyweights like Reliance Industries and Kotak Mahindra Bank were among the top drags on the indices, contributing significantly to the day's losses. Their performance underscores the impact of sector-specific challenges and broader market sentiment on even the most established players.

In contrast to the equity market's performance, Indian government bonds witnessed their fifth consecutive session of gains. This surge was primarily driven by an easing of US-Iran tensions (which, paradoxically, weighed on equities due to profit booking on prior gains related to easing tensions) and robust foreign investment inflows. The 10-year yield fell to its lowest point since March, reflecting increased demand for sovereign debt. However, the gains were somewhat limited by lingering concerns over a widening monsoon deficit in the central region, which poses a risk of food inflation and could temper market enthusiasm in the coming weeks.

RBI Updates: India's External Debt on the Rise

In a significant update from the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), the nation's external debt has shown an increase, reaching US$ 762.8 billion at the end of March 2026. This represents an increase of US$ 26.3 billion over its level at end-March 2025. Consequently, India's external debt to GDP ratio rose to 20.8 per cent at end-March 2026, up from 19.8 per cent at end-March 2025.

The RBI report clarified that a substantial portion of this increase, specifically US$ 24.6 billion, was attributed to the valuation effect. This effect arose due to the appreciation of the US dollar vis-à-vis the Indian rupee and other major currencies. Excluding this valuation effect, the external debt would have increased by a more substantial US$ 51.0 billion. While the overall increase is notable, the impact of currency valuation highlights the global economic factors at play in India's financial position.

This rise in external debt, while being closely monitored, is generally seen as manageable given India's robust foreign exchange reserves and economic growth trajectory. However, it remains a key metric for analysts and policymakers alike, influencing credit ratings and investor confidence.
